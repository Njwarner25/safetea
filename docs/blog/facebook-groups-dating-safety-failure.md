# Why Facebook Groups Fail at Dating Safety (And What's Replacing Them)

The "Are We Dating The Same Guy" movement was one of the most significant grassroots safety initiatives in the history of online dating. What started as a single Facebook group quickly expanded into thousands of city-specific communities where women shared information about potentially dangerous or dishonest people they had encountered on dating apps. At its peak, AWDTSG groups existed in virtually every major city in the United States and had spread internationally.

The movement proved something important: people desperately want community-driven safety tools for dating. But it also exposed everything wrong with using Facebook groups as safety infrastructure.

## The Rise of AWDTSG

The concept was simple. If you matched with someone on a dating app, you could post their profile in your local AWDTSG group and ask if anyone had experience with them. Members would respond with their own stories, warnings about red flags, or confirmations that the person was genuine.

For many women, these groups were the first time they had access to any kind of community-sourced safety information about the people they were dating. The demand was overwhelming. Groups grew to tens of thousands of members within weeks of launching. The movement filled a gap that dating apps themselves had ignored for years.

## Where Facebook Groups Break Down

Despite the good intentions behind AWDTSG, the Facebook group model has fundamental problems that make it unsuitable for safety-critical information.

**Deletions without warning.** Facebook has repeatedly shut down AWDTSG groups with no notice, often in response to mass reporting campaigns. When a group with 50,000 members gets deleted overnight, all the safety information it contained vanishes. Reports about dangerous individuals, warnings about active scammers, and patterns of predatory behavior simply disappear. Members have to start from scratch in a new group, assuming they can even find it.

**Screenshot leaks and privacy failures.** Facebook groups have no real mechanism to prevent screenshots. Posts meant for a private safety community regularly end up on Twitter, Reddit, or sent directly to the person being discussed. This creates real danger for the people sharing their experiences. Women have reported harassment, threats, and even stalking after their AWDTSG posts were leaked.

**Moderator burnout.** Running an AWDTSG group is a volunteer job with no compensation, no tools, and enormous emotional toll. Moderators review hundreds of posts daily, many describing abuse, assault, and traumatic experiences. Without professional support or adequate moderation tools, burnout is inevitable. When moderators step away, groups quickly devolve into drama, off-topic posting, or outright misinformation.

**Legal vulnerability.** Facebook groups offer no legal framework for the safety information being shared. A post that crosses the line from personal experience into defamation exposes both the poster and the group administrators to legal action. Several AWDTSG moderators have faced lawsuits or legal threats, with no institutional support from Facebook.

**No verification.** Anyone can join a Facebook group and post anything. There is no identity verification, no way to confirm that a poster is sharing a genuine experience, and no mechanism to flag false reports. This lack of verification undermines the credibility of the entire system and creates opportunities for malicious use.

## The App Store Problem

The problems extend beyond Facebook. When developers tried to build standalone AWDTSG-style apps, they ran into platform resistance. At least one dating safety app was pulled from the Google Play Store over concerns about content policies. Building safety tools on platforms you do not control means those platforms can shut you down at any time for any reason.
