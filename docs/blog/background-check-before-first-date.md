# The Complete Guide to Running a Background Check Before a Date

Running a background check on someone before a first date used to feel extreme. Now it is increasingly common. A 2025 survey from the Pew Research Center found that nearly 40 percent of online daters have searched for public records on a match before meeting in person. The question is no longer whether you should screen a potential date, but how to do it responsibly.

This guide walks through the process step by step, explains what you can and cannot legally find, and clarifies the line between safety and overreach.

## What You Can Legally Find

Background checks for personal use draw from publicly available records. Here is what is typically accessible.

**Court Records.** Most criminal court records are public in the United States. You can search for felony and misdemeanor convictions, pending cases, and in many jurisdictions, arrest records. County court websites often have free search tools, though the coverage varies widely.

**Sex Offender Registry.** Every state maintains a sex offender registry that is fully searchable by the public. The National Sex Offender Public Website (NSOPW) aggregates data from all 50 states into a single search tool. This is free and should be a baseline step for anyone meeting a stranger from the internet.

**Civil Court Records.** Restraining orders, protection orders, and civil suits are often publicly accessible. These can reveal patterns of behavior that criminal records alone might not show.

**Mugshot Databases.** Some jurisdictions publish booking photos online. While these should be interpreted carefully since an arrest does not equal a conviction, they can help verify identity.

## Understanding FCRA Compliance

The Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) governs how background check data can be used. Here is what you need to know as a consumer.

**Personal use is legal.** You have every right to search public records for your own safety. The FCRA primarily regulates background checks used for employment, housing, and credit decisions.

**You cannot use results to discriminate.** Background check data obtained for personal safety should not be used to make employment or housing decisions about someone.

**Accuracy varies.** Public records databases are not perfect. Records may be outdated, incomplete, or may belong to someone with a similar name. Never make a final judgment based solely on a background check. Use it as one data point among many.

**Some records are sealed or expunged.** Juvenile records, expunged convictions, and sealed cases will typically not appear in a background check. This is by legal design and is meant to allow for rehabilitation.

## Step-by-Step: Running a Check

**Step 1: Gather basic information.** You need at minimum a full name. A phone number, approximate age, or city improves accuracy significantly. Most people have this information from a dating app profile.

**Step 2: Start with free resources.** Search the National Sex Offender Public Website. Check your county's court records website. Google the person's full name in quotes along with their city.

**Step 3: Use a consolidated tool.** Services like SafeTea aggregate multiple databases into a single search, saving you the time of checking each source individually and reducing the chance you miss something.

**Step 4: Verify identity.** Make sure the records you find actually belong to your match. Common names create false positives. Cross-reference age, location, and any other identifying details before drawing conclusions.

**Step 5: Interpret results carefully.** A clean background check does not guarantee safety. Many harmful behaviors never result in criminal charges. Conversely, a single old misdemeanor does not necessarily make someone dangerous. Context matters.

## Red Flags in Background Check Results

Certain findings should give you serious pause. Multiple convictions for violent offenses indicate a pattern, not a one-time mistake. Active restraining orders from former partners are significant warning signs. Sex offender registry entries require careful consideration of the offense and circumstances. A history of fraud or identity-related offenses suggests dishonesty that extends beyond a single incident.

## When Not to Run a Background Check

Background checks are safety tools, not surveillance tools. Do not use them to dig up dirt on someone you are already in a healthy relationship with out of insecurity. Do not run checks on someone to gain leverage or ammunition. And do not share background check results publicly or use them to harass someone.

This is about your safety, not judgment. Everyone has a past, and a background check is simply one tool for making informed decisions about who you meet in person.
