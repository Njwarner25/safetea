### H2: 1. The profile has only one photo (and it's flawless)
- A single photo, especially one that looks professionally shot or studio-quality, is the single most common pattern in fake profiles.
- Why it matters: real profiles are messy. They include candids, group shots, awkward angles. A profile with one perfect shot is the digital equivalent of an unmarked van.

### H2: 2. The bio is suspiciously generic — or suspiciously polished
- "Looking for my queen / king," "Just here for vibes," and other template lines appear in fake profiles at much higher rates than real ones.
- The opposite is also a flag: copy that reads like ad copy, with no personality, no specifics, and no friction.

### H2: 3. Photos that don't match each other
- Different lighting, different cameras, different ages of the same person — sometimes pulled from a stranger's Instagram or stock photo library.
- Reverse image search the photos. (Internal link to post #4 once it's published.)

### H2: 4. The age and the vibe don't match
- A 27-year-old's bio that reads like a 45-year-old wrote it (or vice versa) is a tell. Slang, references, and writing rhythm are hard to fake convincingly.

### H2: 5. No connected Instagram, Spotify, or other accounts
- Tinder lets profiles link to Instagram and Spotify. Real profiles often do; fake profiles rarely can without breaking the illusion.
- Caveat: privacy-conscious users sometimes link nothing on purpose. Weight this signal against the others, do not treat it as standalone proof.

### H2: 6. They want to leave Tinder immediately
- First message is "let's text on WhatsApp" or "I barely use this app, here's my number."
- Why it matters: Tinder has moderation and reporting tools. A scammer's first move is to get you off Tinder and onto a channel where the platform can't see what happens next.

### H2: 7. Love-bombing in the first 24 hours
- "I feel like I've known you forever," "you're so different from everyone else on here," "I'm done with the apps now that I found you."
- This is a manipulation pattern, not enthusiasm. Real connection takes longer than two days to form.

### H2: 8. They refuse to video chat
- A short video call before meeting in person is the cheapest verification step you have. A match who has excuses for every video request is telling you something.
- Most catfish profiles cannot pass a video call. That is why they avoid it.

### H2: 9. Money comes up early
- Any mention of cryptocurrency, investment opportunities, financial hardship, "my account is locked," or a sob story that ends with a request — flag it immediately.
- The FTC reported $X billion in romance-scam losses in the most recent year. `<<VERIFY: latest FTC romance scam losses figure>>`

### H2: 10. The profile feels too "on" for you specifically
- Every interest matches yours. Every photo is in places you also love. The bio reads like it was written for your dating profile in particular.
- Reason: scammers and serial manipulators often read your profile carefully and mirror it back. Real matches share *some* overlap, not all of it.

### H2: 11. Brand-new account with very few matches' worth of polish
- Profiles created in the last 24–48 hours with one or two photos and a thin bio are statistically more likely to be throwaways or scam accounts.
- Not a verdict on its own — everyone is new at some point — but a meaningful flag in combination.

### H2: 12. Your gut is telling you something
- Worth saying directly. The pattern recognition that is making you uneasy is real, even when you cannot articulate it. The point of this list is to help you put words to instincts you already have.

### H2: How to verify before you meet
- Three specific moves: reverse image search the photos, ask for a short video call, run the profile through a screening tool that aggregates patterns automatically.

## FAQ section (FAQ schema)

Render with FAQPage JSON-LD and as visible H3s.

**Q1: How common are fake profiles on Tinder?**
> Tinder removes hundreds of thousands of suspicious accounts per quarter through automated systems, and many more get through. Estimates of the percentage of fake or scam profiles vary widely, but most independent research puts it between 5% and 15% at any given time. The percentage of misleading-but-real profiles — exaggerated photos, outdated info, undisclosed relationship status — is much higher.

**Q2: Is one red flag enough to unmatch?**
> Not usually. A single flag often has a benign explanation. The pattern that matters is two or more flags appearing together — for example, a single photo plus a generic bio plus an immediate push to text. That combination is much more predictive than any individual signal.

**Q3: What is the single most reliable way to verify someone is real?**
> A short video call before meeting in person. Most fake profiles and catfish accounts cannot pass even a brief video chat. If your match has excuses every time you suggest one, that is meaningful information by itself.

**Q4: Can I report a profile that just feels off, even if I have no proof?**
> Yes. Tinder lets you report profiles based on suspected fakes, scams, or unsafe behavior, and the platform reviews reports without requiring you to provide evidence. Reports also feed Tinder's automated moderation systems, so flagging a suspicious profile helps protect other users even if it is not removed for you.

**Q5: How do I check if a Tinder profile's photos are stolen?**
> Save one of the profile photos and run it through a reverse image search — Google Images, TinEye, and dedicated tools all work. If the photo appears on someone else's social media, a stock photo site, or in articles unrelated to the person on Tinder, you have your answer.
